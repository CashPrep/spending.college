How Much Money Do You Need?
How much spending money does a college student need per month? ▼

Most college students need between $800 and $2,200 per month for personal expenses, depending on their state, housing type, and lifestyle. This number excludes tuition, mandatory fees, and health insurance. Students living off-campus in major cities like New York, LA, or Boston typically land at the higher end. Students sharing an apartment in the Midwest or South can often get by on $800–$1,100/month. Use the state-specific calculator to get a personalized estimate for your school location.

Is $1,000 a month enough for a college student? ▼

It depends heavily on location. In low-cost states like Ohio, Indiana, Iowa, or Mississippi, $1,000/month is workable if housing is shared and you cook most meals. But in California, New York, Massachusetts, or Washington, $1,000 won't cover rent alone in many cities. The national average for student personal expenses runs around $1,200–$1,400/month when you include housing, food, and transportation. Check our state guides for a breakdown by region.

What is a reasonable college student budget? ▼

A realistic baseline budget for most students (excluding tuition) looks like this:

  • Housing: $400–$900/month
  • Food & Groceries: $250–$450/month
  • Transportation: $50–$200/month
  • Personal & Miscellaneous: $100–$250/month
  • Phone: $30–$80/month
  • Entertainment: $50–$150/month

Total: roughly $880–$2,030/month depending on location and lifestyle choices.

How much money should parents give college students per month? ▼

The right amount depends on what parents are expected to cover and what the student earns themselves. Families who cover all non-tuition expenses typically send between $500 and $1,500/month. The best approach is to use our calculator to get the actual monthly cost for your student's state, then subtract any income from part-time work. That gives you a specific, data-backed number rather than a guess. Most financial aid offices also have cost-of-attendance estimates that can serve as a reference.

Saving Money in College
What is the biggest expense for most college students? ▼

Housing is by far the largest single expense for most college students, typically representing 40–55% of total monthly spending. This is why where you live — and who you live with — makes the biggest difference in your overall budget. Students who share a 3- or 4-bedroom apartment with roommates can cut housing costs by $300–600/month compared to living alone or in a private dorm room.

How do college students save money on food? ▼

The highest-impact food strategies are: cooking meals in bulk on Sundays (saves $150–$250/month vs. eating out), shopping at Aldi or Trader Joe's instead of standard grocery chains, using every available meal plan swipe instead of buying food separately, and eliminating daily coffee shop visits (~$80–$120/month savings). Students who meal-prep consistently spend an average of $180–$280/month on food compared to $400–$600 for those who eat out frequently.

How can I live on a tight budget in college? ▼

The three highest-leverage moves for a tight budget are: (1) live with 3+ roommates to split rent, (2) don't bring a car to campus (saves $250–$450/month in gas, insurance, and parking), and (3) cook at home instead of eating out. Beyond that, max out every free campus resource — gym, printing, tutoring, counseling — you're already paying for them through fees. On-Campus vs. Off-Campus
Is it cheaper to live on campus or off campus? ▼

It depends on the school and city, but off-campus is cheaper more often than not — especially after your first year. Dorms typically cost $800–$1,400/month when you include mandatory meal plan costs. A shared off-campus apartment can often be had for $400–$700/person. The tradeoff is convenience and the time cost of cooking and commuting. See our detailed on-campus vs. off-campus cost comparison for a state-by-state breakdown.

Do you need a meal plan in college? ▼

Freshmen living in dorms are usually required to purchase a meal plan — it's written into housing contracts at most schools. After freshman year, it's optional. Whether to keep it depends on cost per meal versus what you'd spend cooking. If a 10-meal/week plan works out to under $8/meal, it's often worth keeping. If it's $12–$15/meal (common at many schools), cooking at home will almost always be cheaper.

Jobs & Income
Can college students work part-time and still do well in school? ▼

Yes — research consistently shows that students who work up to 15–20 hours per week have similar or slightly better GPAs than those who don't work at all, likely because work forces better time management. Beyond 20–25 hours/week, academic performance tends to dip. The ideal is a job that fits around your class schedule — on-campus jobs, remote freelance work, and gig work (tutoring, delivery) are the most schedule-friendly options. Browse part-time jobs for students on our jobs page.

What are the best side hustles for college students? ▼

The highest-earning side hustles for college students in 2026 are:

  • Tutoring: $20–$60/hour, especially in STEM subjects
  • Freelance writing or social media: $15–$40/hour
  • Campus brand ambassador: $15–$25/hour + perks
  • Food delivery (DoorDash, Uber Eats): Fully flexible, $15–$25/hour net
  • Selling notes or study guides: Passive income, minimal time
