College Money Guides

Simple, honest guidance to help you spend less, earn what you need, and use your College Cash estimate in real life. This page is information only, not personal financial advice.

1. Use your College Cash number tap to open/close

When you use the College Cash calculator, you get a yearly and monthly spending estimate based on your state, housing, meal setup, and lifestyle choices.

  • Take the monthly estimate and divide it by 4 to get a weekly target. For example, $400 per month is about $100 per week.
  • Use that weekly number as your “all in” budget for food out, rides, fun, random purchases, and extras.
  • Plug the same monthly number into the College Job Hours calculator to see how many hours of work you need at different pay rates.
  • Re‑run both tools when something changes — like moving off campus, dropping your meal plan, or taking on more classes.

2. Saving money in college tap to open/close

You cannot control every cost, but small choices add up. These tips are based on common patterns seen in student budget research and reports from campus financial aid offices, not on any one school’s rules.

Food and meal plans

  • Treat your meal plan like a pre‑paid card. Use most of your swipes or dining dollars before buying extra meals off campus.
  • Keep low‑effort food in your room (instant oatmeal, pasta, frozen meals, snacks) so you are not ordering delivery by default.
  • If you do not have a meal plan, cook simple meals in batches and share grocery costs with roommates whenever possible.
  • Track how often you order out in one week; even cutting one delivery or restaurant trip can free up money every month.

Textbooks and class materials

  • Compare prices: campus bookstore, used copies, rentals, older editions, and official digital versions can all be cheaper than new print.
  • Ask your professor if an older edition is acceptable. Often the content is similar but much less expensive.
  • Use the library when you can. Some classes keep copies of the textbook on reserve for short‑term use.

Transportation and getting around

  • Learn your campus transit options. Many colleges include local buses or shuttles in student fees at no extra charge.
  • Walk or bike for short trips when it is safe instead of using rideshares by default.
  • If you drive, try to group errands into one trip and split parking or gas costs with friends when it makes sense.

Subscriptions and “small” expenses

  • List every subscription you pay for (streaming, apps, cloud storage, games). Cancel anything you barely use.
  • Look for student discounts on services you truly need; many large companies offer lower student pricing.
  • Decide on a monthly “fun” amount for things like coffee, snacks, and in‑app purchases and stick to it.
3. Finding jobs while in college tap to open/close

The right job depends on your schedule, financial aid, and what is available on your campus and online. These ideas are based on common job types many colleges and students talk about.

On‑campus jobs

  • Check your school’s official job board or career center website for positions like library assistant, front desk staff, lab helper, or event support.
  • Ask departments related to your major about openings for office assistants, graders, or research assistants.
  • Campus jobs often understand student schedules and may be more flexible around exams than off‑campus work.

Work‑study and financial aid‑linked jobs

If your financial aid package mentions “work‑study,” that usually means you may qualify for certain part‑time jobs funded through a government or school program. Exact rules depend on the country and school.

  • Read your financial aid letter carefully and contact your financial aid office if you are not sure whether you have work‑study.
  • Ask which campus offices are hiring work‑study students and how to apply through your school.

Off‑campus and remote jobs

  • Look for part‑time roles with flexible hours, such as retail, food service, childcare, or front desk work near campus.
  • For remote work, search large job sites for terms like “part‑time remote,” “evening shift,” or roles in customer support, content review, or online tutoring.
  • Be cautious of any job that asks you to pay money up front, share sensitive personal information too early, or move money for other people. If something feels off, ask your campus career center for a second opinion.

Balancing hours with school

  • Many students aim for somewhere around 10–20 hours of work per week during the semester, but the right number depends on your course load and personal situation.
  • Use the College Job Hours calculator to see how different hourly rates and hours per week line up with your spending goal.
  • If your grades start to slip or you feel overwhelmed, talk to an advisor and consider adjusting your hours.

4. A simple student budget tap to open/close

You do not need a perfect budget app to be organized. A basic structure can make your College Cash estimate much more useful.

  1. Start with your monthly College Cash number (from the calculator) and list your expected income for the month.
  2. Separate essentials (transportation, phone bill, basic groceries, hygiene items) from extras (eating out, fun, shopping).
  3. Make sure your essentials fit comfortably inside your income, then decide how much of the remaining money you want to use for extras and how much to save.
  4. Check in once a week. You can use a notes app, spreadsheet, or any budgeting app you like; the tool is less important than being consistent.
5. Helpful places to look for more info tap to open/close

These are general types of resources many students use. Always use official school or government websites when you are making big decisions.

  • Your own college career center site and job board (log in through your school’s official website or student portal).

  • Major job boards for part-time and remote roles, such as Indeed and LinkedIn Jobs .
  • Large scholarship search tools run by well-known organizations, for example Fastweb or College Board’s BigFuture .
