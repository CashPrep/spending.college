Understanding the Types of Financial Aid

Financial aid comes in four main forms. The single most important thing to understand is that grants and scholarships are free money — you never pay them back. Loans must be repaid, with interest. Work-study gives you a job, not a check.

🎁
Grants Free money

Need-based aid you don't repay. The Pell Grant (federal) gives up to $7,395/year to qualifying students. State grants add additional money on top. Always maximize grants before borrowing.

🏆
Scholarships Free money

Merit or application-based aid you don't repay. Available from your college, outside organizations, employers, local community foundations, and hundreds of niche programs. There is no limit to how many you can apply for.

💼
Work-Study You earn it

A federal program that subsidizes on-campus or nonprofit jobs for eligible students. Typical earnings are $2,500–$4,000/year. The money goes directly to you (not your tuition bill) — great for living expenses.

📋
Loans Must repay

Borrowed money you repay with interest after graduation. Direct Subsidized loans (interest-free while in school) are better than Unsubsidized. Both are better than PLUS or private loans. Borrow as little as possible.

FAFSA: The Most Important Form You'll Fill Out

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) determines your eligibility for virtually all federal aid — grants, loans, and work-study. Many state and institutional scholarships also require a completed FAFSA. Filing it is mandatory if you want any aid.

Key FAFSA Facts for 2025–2026

  • Opens: October 1 each year for the following academic year
  • Federal deadline: June 30, but most state and school deadlines are February–March — file early
  • Filing is free at studentaid.gov — never pay a service to fill it out
  • You must renew it every year — your aid package can change based on your family's income
  • You need a StudentAid.gov account (FSA ID) — create yours and your parent's before starting

What the FAFSA Calculates: Student Aid Index (SAI)

The FAFSA calculates a Student Aid Index (SAI), formerly called Expected Family Contribution (EFC). A lower SAI means more need-based aid eligibility. A SAI of $0 means maximum Pell Grant eligibility. If your family's income changed significantly this year (job loss, divorce, medical bills), you may be able to get a professional judgment review that adjusts your aid — more on that below.

💡 Pro tip: File your FAFSA as early as possible after October 1. Many states have first-come, first-served aid programs — waiting until spring can cost you thousands in state grants that ran out.

The annual borrowing limits for undergraduates are $5,500 (year 1), $6,500 (year 2), and $7,500 (years 3–4) for dependent students. Independent students can borrow more. These limits exist for good reason — stay under them if possible by maximizing grants and scholarships first.

Scholarships You Can Still Apply For in College

Most students think scholarship season ends when they get accepted to college. It doesn't. Millions of dollars in scholarship money goes unclaimed every year because current college students stop looking. Here's where to find them:

Institutional Scholarships (Most Valuable)

Your own college likely has dozens of scholarships administered by its foundation or individual departments. Check with your financial aid office, academic department, and the scholarship database on your school's website. These are less competitive than national scholarships because fewer students know to apply.

Local and Community Scholarships

Community foundations, Rotary clubs, local businesses, and civic organizations in your hometown offer scholarships exclusively to students from your area. These often have only 10–50 applicants. Search “[your city/county] scholarship [current year]” to find them.

National Databases

  • Fastweb.com — Free; matches scholarships to your profile
  • Scholarships.com — Large database with niche categories
  • Bold.org — Growing database; some scholarships are essay-free
  • Your state's higher education agency — Lists state-funded awards

Niche Scholarships Worth Knowing

There are scholarships for nearly every interest, background, and career path: left-handed students, students who collect duck decoys, students studying abroad in specific countries, students entering specific industries. The more niche it is, the less competition you face.

💡 Pro tip: Treat scholarship applications like a part-time job. If you spend 5 hours writing a strong, reusable scholarship essay and win a $1,000 award, you just earned $200/hour. Write one great essay, then adapt it for multiple applications.

How to Appeal Your Financial Aid Package

Most students don't realize they can appeal their financial aid offer — and that it works more often than you'd think. If your family's financial situation changed or your aid package doesn't reflect your actual need, you have every right to ask for a review.

Valid Reasons to Appeal

  • Parent lost a job or had a significant income reduction
  • Family has high medical expenses not reflected on tax returns
  • Parents recently divorced or separated
  • You received a better offer from a comparable college (leverage)
  • Your family has unusual expenses (elder care, private K-12 costs for a sibling)

How to Write an Appeal Letter

Call or email the financial aid office and ask about their professional judgment or appeal process. Then write a brief, honest letter (1–2 paragraphs) that: (1) states your specific situation, (2) provides documentation (employer letter, medical bill, other school's award letter), and (3) asks for a specific review. Be polite and specific — vague appeals rarely succeed. Emotional appeals without documentation also rarely work.

Appealing With a Competing Offer

If School A offers you $18,000 and School B (a comparable school you prefer) offers $12,000, you can contact School B's financial aid office, share School A's award letter, and ask if they can match or improve it. This works most effectively at schools with similar rankings and selectivity. Schools want to enroll you — they sometimes will negotiate.

Income-Driven Repayment and Loan Forgiveness

If you do borrow, understanding your repayment options before you graduate prevents a lot of financial stress. Federal loans have several income-driven repayment (IDR) plans that cap your monthly payment at 5–10% of your discretionary income.

  • SAVE Plan — The newest IDR plan; caps payments at 5% of discretionary income for undergrad loans; balances forgiven after 20–25 years
  • Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) — If you work for a government agency or 501(c)(3) nonprofit for 10 years while making payments, your remaining balance is forgiven tax-free
  • Teacher Loan Forgiveness — Up to $17,500 forgiven if you teach in a low-income school for 5 consecutive years

These programs have specific enrollment requirements. Check studentaid.gov for current program details, as rules have changed frequently in recent years.
